Height and Wind:

These parameters involve two critical components: the height at a specific pressure level in the atmosphere and the characteristics of the wind at that level. These are vital for understanding atmospheric circulation patterns, including jet streams, troughs, and ridges.

Example:

  • 500 mb Height, Wind: The geopotential height and wind characteristics at the 500 millibar (mb) pressure level. This helps meteorologists identify mid-level weather patterns, which can influence surface weather.

Temperature and Wind (500 mb, 700 mb, 850 mb, 925 mb):

These parameters provide information about both temperature and wind characteristics at different pressure levels. Analyzing temperature profiles along with wind patterns helps meteorologists assess stability, moisture distribution, and the potential for weather phenomena.

Example:

  • 850 mb Temperature, Height, Wind Barbs: This parameter combines temperature, atmospheric height, and wind barbs at the 850 mb pressure level. It's valuable for understanding temperature trends and wind patterns in the lower atmosphere.

2 m AGL Relative Humidity:

This parameter represents the relative humidity at a height of 2 meters above ground level. Relative humidity is the percentage of moisture in the air relative to its maximum capacity at a given temperature.

Significance:

Higher relative humidity levels suggest greater moisture availability, which can influence cloud formation, precipitation, and potential for severe weather events.

2 m AGL Temperature:

This parameter represents the air temperature at a height of 2 meters above ground level. It provides valuable information about near-surface weather conditions.

Significance:

Air temperature affects daily weather conditions and is essential for understanding comfort levels and potential for temperature-related impacts.

2 m AGL Dew Point:

The dew point represents the temperature at which air becomes saturated with moisture, leading to condensation and potentially the formation of dew or frost.

Significance:

Dew point is a critical factor for predicting fog, frost, and the potential for condensation and cloud formation.

1 km AGL Reflectivity with Precip Type:

This parameter represents the reflectivity of radar echoes at a height of 1 kilometer above ground level. Reflectivity indicates the intensity of precipitation.

Significance:

Reflectivity helps meteorologists assess the intensity of precipitation and identify different types of precipitation, such as rain, snow, or hail.

Precipitable Water:

Precipitable water represents the amount of water vapor present in a column of the atmosphere. It's a measure of moisture availability.

Significance:

Precipitable water helps meteorologists gauge the potential for rainfall and assess the overall moisture content of the atmosphere.

Composite Reflectivity:

Composite reflectivity combines radar echoes from different angles, providing a comprehensive view of precipitation intensity and distribution.

Significance:

This parameter helps meteorologists understand the extent and intensity of precipitation events across a specific area.

Storm Relative Helicity (SRH), Energy Helicity Index (EHI), Supercell Composite, Significant Tornado Parameter (SigTor):

These parameters provide insights into the potential for severe weather events such as tornadoes and supercell thunderstorms.

Significance:

These parameters assess conditions that contribute to organized and potentially dangerous weather phenomena, helping meteorologists issue warnings and forecasts to keep the public safe.

Lapse Rate, CAPE, CIN:

These parameters relate to atmospheric stability and convective potential.

Significance:

Lapse rate reflects temperature changes with height and affects the potential for vertical air motion. CAPE indicates the energy available for convection, while CIN represents inhibition to convection. These parameters are essential for predicting thunderstorms and other convective events.

Frontogenesis, Temperature Advection:

These parameters help meteorologists understand the development and movement of temperature gradients and weather fronts.

Significance:

Frontogenesis and temperature advection aid in identifying areas where temperature changes are occurring due to the movement of air masses, which influence weather patterns.

The models and their associated parameters provide meteorologists with a comprehensive toolbox for analyzing weather conditions at different altitudes and understanding the potential for various weather phenomena. These parameters are crucial for making accurate forecasts and predictions, issuing timely warnings, and ultimately ensuring public safety in the face of changing weather patterns.
