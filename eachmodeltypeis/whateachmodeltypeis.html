Height and Wind:

  • These parameters provide information about the height of a specific pressure level in the atmosphere (usually in meters above sea level) and the wind characteristics at that level.
  • Wind is typically represented by its speed and direction (wind vector).
  • These parameters help meteorologists understand the vertical profile of wind and atmospheric pressure, which is crucial for analyzing atmospheric circulation patterns and identifying features like jet streams, troughs, and ridges.

200 mb Height, Wind:

  • Represents the height of the 200 millibar (mb) pressure level in the atmosphere and the wind characteristics at that level.
  • The 200 mb level is located high in the atmosphere and is often associated with the upper-level jet stream.
  • Provides insight into the strength and direction of the jet stream, which influences the movement of weather systems.

300 mb Height, Wind:

  • Represents the height of the 300 mb pressure level in the atmosphere and the wind characteristics at that level.
  • The 300 mb level is also located in the upper atmosphere and contributes to understanding upper-level wind patterns and their effects on weather systems.

500 mb Height, Wind:

  • Represents the height of the 500 mb pressure level in the atmosphere and the wind characteristics at that level.
  • The 500 mb level is a crucial indicator of the mid-level flow and is often used to identify the position of troughs and ridges in the atmosphere, which influence surface weather patterns.

700 mb Height, Wind:

  • Represents the height of the 700 mb pressure level in the atmosphere and the wind characteristics at that level.
  • The 700 mb level is significant for understanding the flow of air masses and identifying features that contribute to the development of precipitation.

850 mb Height, Wind:

  • Represents the height of the 850 mb pressure level in the atmosphere and the wind characteristics at that level.
  • The 850 mb level is important for assessing low-level atmospheric conditions, which impact surface temperatures, humidity, and weather patterns.

925 mb Height, Wind:

  • Represents the height of the 925 mb pressure level in the atmosphere and the wind characteristics at that level.
  • The 925 mb level is closer to the surface and provides information about the lower atmosphere, which can influence temperature, moisture, and weather conditions.

Temperature and Wind (500 mb, 700 mb, 850 mb, 925 mb):

  • These parameters provide information about both the temperature and wind characteristics at specific pressure levels (500 mb, 700 mb, 850 mb, and 925 mb).
  • Understanding the temperature profile along with wind patterns helps in analyzing stability, moisture distribution, and potential for weather phenomena like precipitation and convection.

2 m AGL Relative Humidity:

  • Represents the relative humidity at a height of 2 meters above ground level (AGL).
  • Indicates the percentage of moisture in the air relative to the maximum amount of moisture the air can hold at that temperature.

2 m AGL Temperature:

  • Represents the air temperature at a height of 2 meters AGL.
  • Provides information about the temperature near the Earth's surface, which is important for understanding daily weather conditions.

2 m AGL Temperature, Wind Barbs:

  • Combines the 2 m AGL temperature with wind barbs to show both temperature and wind direction/speed at the same level.

2 m AGL Wind Chill/Heat Index:

  • Represents either the wind chill (in cold conditions) or the heat index (in warm conditions) at a height of 2 meters AGL.
  • Takes into account the combined effect of temperature and wind (for wind chill) or temperature and humidity (for heat index) on how humans perceive the weather.

2 m AGL Dew Point:

  • Represents the dew point temperature at a height of 2 meters AGL.
  • The dew point is the temperature at which the air becomes saturated with moisture, leading to condensation and potentially the formation of dew or frost.

2 m AGL Dew Point, Wind Barbs:

  • Similar to the 2 m AGL Temperature Wind Barbs, this parameter combines the 2 m AGL dew point with wind barbs to provide both dew point and wind information.

2 m AGL Theta-e, Wind Barbs:

  • Represents the equivalent potential temperature (theta-e) at a height of 2 meters AGL, combined with wind barbs.
  • Equivalent potential temperature is a quantity that combines temperature, moisture, and pressure information to assess air parcel stability and predict thunderstorm potential.

MSLP, 10 m AGL Wind:

  • Represents mean sea level pressure (MSLP) and wind information at a height of 10 meters above ground level (AGL).
  • MSLP is adjusted to sea level conditions, and the wind information provides the speed and direction of the wind near the surface.

1 km AGL Reflectivity with Precip Type:

  • Represents the reflectivity of radar echoes at a height of 1 kilometer AGL.
  • Reflectivity provides information about the intensity of precipitation, and the parameter may also include precipitation type information (rain, snow, etc.).

3-h QPF, 6-h QPF, 12-h QPF, 24-h QPF, 48-h QPF, Total QPF:

  • These parameters represent quantitative precipitation forecasts (QPF) for different time intervals.
  • QPF indicates the amount of expected precipitation over the specified time period, aiding in predicting rainfall or snowfall amounts.

Cloud Cover:

  • Represents the amount or fraction of sky covered by clouds.
  • Provides information about sky conditions, which can influence temperature and other weather conditions.

Precipitable Water:

  • Represents the amount of water vapor present in a column of the atmosphere.
  • Precipitable water is a measure of moisture availability and can impact the potential for precipitation.

Composite Reflectivity:

  • Represents the composite radar reflectivity, which is a combination of radar echoes from multiple angles.
  • Offers a comprehensive view of precipitation intensity and distribution.

200 mb Relative Humidity, Wind:

  • Represents the relative humidity and wind characteristics at the 200 millibar (mb) pressure level.
  • Provides insight into the moisture content and wind patterns high in the atmosphere, which influence upper-level weather systems.

300 mb Relative Humidity, Wind:

  • Represents the relative humidity and wind characteristics at the 300 mb pressure level.
  • Similar to the 200 mb level, this parameter provides information about upper-level moisture and wind patterns.

500 mb Relative Humidity, Wind:

  • Represents the relative humidity and wind characteristics at the 500 mb pressure level.
  • Helps meteorologists understand moisture availability and atmospheric circulation patterns at a mid-level altitude.

700 mb Relative Humidity, Wind:

  • Represents the relative humidity and wind characteristics at the 700 mb pressure level.
  • Provides information about moisture levels and wind patterns in the lower to mid-levels of the atmosphere.

850 mb Relative Humidity, Wind:

  • Represents the relative humidity and wind characteristics at the 850 mb pressure level.
  • Offers insights into moisture distribution and wind patterns in the lower atmosphere, which can affect surface weather conditions.

925 mb Relative Humidity, Wind:

  • Represents the relative humidity and wind characteristics at the 925 mb pressure level.
  • Gives information about moisture levels and wind patterns in the lower atmosphere, closer to the surface.

850 mb Dew Point, Height, Wind Barbs:

  • Combines the dew point, height, and wind barbs at the 850 mb pressure level.
  • Provides information about the dew point temperature, atmospheric height, and wind characteristics at this level.

925 mb Dew Point, Height, Wind Barbs:

  • Combines the dew point, height, and wind barbs at the 925 mb pressure level.
  • Offers information about the dew point temperature, atmospheric height, and wind characteristics at this level.

500 mb Height, Vorticity:

  • Represents the geopotential height (altitude) at the 500 millibar (mb) pressure level along with the vorticity at that level.
  • Vorticity indicates the rotation of air in the atmosphere and can help identify regions of cyclonic (counterclockwise) and anticyclonic (clockwise) rotation.

700 mb Height, Vorticity:

  • Represents the geopotential height at the 700 mb pressure level along with the vorticity at that level.
  • This parameter aids in understanding vorticity patterns in the middle levels of the atmosphere.

850 mb Height, Vorticity:

  • Represents the geopotential height at the 850 mb pressure level along with the vorticity at that level.
  • Provides insights into vorticity patterns closer to the surface.

500 mb Height, Vertical Velocity:

  • Represents the geopotential height at the 500 mb pressure level along with the vertical velocity (upward or downward motion of air) at that level.
  • Vertical velocity is important for understanding rising and sinking air motions in the atmosphere.

700 mb Height, Vertical Velocity:

  • Represents the geopotential height at the 700 mb pressure level along with the vertical velocity at that level.
  • This parameter helps meteorologists assess the vertical motion of air masses in the middle atmosphere.

850 mb Height, Vertical Velocity:

  • Represents the geopotential height at the 850 mb pressure level along with the vertical velocity at that level.
  • Offers insights into vertical air motion patterns closer to the surface.

700 mb Frontogenesis:

  • Represents the rate of change of temperature gradient at the 700 mb pressure level.
  • Frontogenesis indicates the development or strengthening of temperature gradients associated with weather fronts.

700 mb Temperature Advection:

  • Represents the advection (transport) of temperature by the wind at the 700 mb pressure level.
  • Temperature advection helps identify regions where temperature changes are occurring due to the movement of air masses.

850 mb Frontogenesis:

  • Represents the rate of change of temperature gradient at the 850 mb pressure level.
  • Similar to 700 mb frontogenesis, this parameter indicates the development or strengthening of temperature gradients associated with fronts.

850 mb Temperature Advection:

  • Represents the advection of temperature by the wind at the 850 mb pressure level.
  • This parameter provides information about temperature changes driven by the movement of air masses at a lower atmospheric level.

Lapse Rate: 700-500 mb:

  • Represents the rate at which temperature changes with height between the 700 and 500 millibar (mb) pressure levels.
  • Lapse rates are important for understanding atmospheric stability and determining the potential for vertical air motion.

Mixed-Layer CAPE (Convective Available Potential Energy):

  • Represents the amount of buoyant energy available for convection within the mixed-layer of the atmosphere.
  • Indicates the potential for air parcels to rise and create convective storms.

Mixed-Layer CIN (Convective Inhibition):

  • Represents the amount of energy needed to overcome inhibition and initiate convection within the mixed-layer.
  • CIN values can indicate whether conditions are favorable or inhibitive for convective storm development.

Most Unstable CAPE:

  • Represents the convective available potential energy calculated for the most unstable parcel of air in the atmosphere.
  • Reflects the potential energy for the most buoyant air parcel to rise and create convection.

Surface-Based CAPE:

  • Represents the convective available potential energy calculated using the surface air parcel as the reference.
  • Provides insight into the potential for surface-based convection.

Surface-Based CIN:

  • Represents the convective inhibition calculated using the surface air parcel as the reference.
  • Indicates the potential for inhibition of surface-based convection.

Surface-Based LCL (Lifted Condensation Level) Height:

  • Represents the height at which an air parcel would first become saturated if lifted from the surface.
  • Helps assess the potential for cloud formation and convection.

Surface-Based Lifted Index:

  • Represents the temperature difference between an air parcel lifted from the surface and the environment at a specific level.
  • Negative values indicate unstable conditions that favor convection.

Bulk Shear: Surface-500 mb:

  • Represents the difference in wind speed and direction between the surface and the 500 mb pressure level.
  • Bulk shear is important for understanding storm organization and potential for severe weather.

Storm Relative Helicity (SRH): 0-1 km AGL, 0-3 km AGL:

  • Represents the vertical rotation of the horizontal wind vectors in a storm-relative environment.
  • SRH is used to assess the potential for rotating updrafts that can lead to severe weather, particularly tornadoes.

Energy Helicity Index (EHI): 0-1 km AGL, 0-3 km AGL:

  • Combines instability (CAPE) and helicity (rotational potential) to assess the potential for severe convective storms.
  • EHI values indicate areas where conditions are conducive to supercell development.

Supercell Composite:

  • Combines various atmospheric parameters to identify regions favorable for supercell thunderstorm development.

Significant Tornado Parameter (SigTor):

  • A composite parameter that indicates the potential for significant tornadoes.
  • Considers multiple factors including instability, wind shear, and helicity.

SBCAPE, 500-850-Sfc Crossover:

  • Represents surface-based CAPE calculated with a parcel originating at the intersection of the 500 mb and 850 mb pressure levels.
  • Provides insight into the potential for severe weather.

SBCAPE, Hodographs:

  • Represents surface-based CAPE calculated using a hodograph, which is a plot of wind speed and direction with height.
  • Helps assess the potential for organized convection.
